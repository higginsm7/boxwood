About This Site

The genus Buxus has shaped formal gardens for over two thousand years — and yet, somehow, no one had made a dedicated Mid-Atlantic boxwood garden website. Until now. You're welcome. Three collections are featured here: a living reference library in the Virginia countryside, a landmark of American botanical science in Washington, D.C., and a colonial garden in Yorktown where the boxwoods have witnessed considerably more history than most shrubs are asked to.

Virginia

Boyce, Clarke County, VA

ABS Memorial Garden
at Blandy Experimental Farm

Founded in 1961 by a group of gardeners deeply alarmed by the spread of boxwood decline throughout Virginia (as one would be), the American Boxwood Society made its permanent home at Blandy's Orland E. White Arboretum. The ABS Memorial Garden — developed in 1974 as a tribute to Society founders Henry Hohman and Dr. John T. Baldwin — has grown into the largest reference collection of its kind, showcasing over 162 labeled cultivars across manicured beds on the arboretum's 172-acre grounds. A serious institution for serious shrub people.

Washington, D.C.

Northeast Washington, D.C.

National Boxwood Collection
U.S. National Arboretum

Established by an act of Congress in 1927 — because some things are simply too important to leave to chance — the U.S. National Arboretum's boxwood holdings are among the most comprehensive in the temperate world. The National Boxwood Collection comprises over 180 taxa and serves as a critical resource for botanical research, plant breeding, and disease resistance studies. It also acts as a backup collection to Blandy, because apparently the nation requires a boxwood redundancy plan.

Virginia

Yorktown, York County, VA

The Boxwood Garden
at Nelson House

Built around 1730 and managed today by the National Park Service, Nelson House is one of the finest surviving Georgian mansions in Virginia — and it has the cannonball scars to prove it. The walled rear garden features a sprawling collection of old boxwoods, planted in the formal colonial tradition and left largely to their own devices, which lends the whole scene a pleasingly wild dignity. The house was famously used as Lord Cornwallis's headquarters during the 1781 Siege of Yorktown, making its garden perhaps the most historically dramatic place in the Mid-Atlantic to admire a shrub.

~1730 House Built

Free Admission

🎖 Notable Garden Feature

Tucked into the garden wall is a bronze memorial plaque to Earl Cornwallis, Commander of the British Forces — erected, with admirable diplomatic spirit, by the Virginia Yorktown Sesquicentennial Commission as "a testimonial of the affection of Virginia for the Mother Country." Whether the boxwoods share this affection is unrecorded.

Living Reference Libraries

The Blandy and National Arboretum collections function as scientific reference libraries — every plant labeled, documented, and available for study. The boxwoods at Nelson House are more of a living history exhibit, but they're doing great.

🤝

Mutual Conservation

Blandy and the National Arboretum serve as mutual backup repositories, ensuring cultivar diversity is protected if disease threatens one site. Two redundant boxwood archives — as nature, and apparently the U.S. Congress, intended.

🌱

Open to All

All three gardens are free and open to the public. There is no admission fee. You can simply walk in and look at boxwoods. Please consider doing this.

About Buxus

A Genus with
Deep Roots

Boxwood — genus Buxus — has been a cornerstone of formal garden design since antiquity. Ancient Romans prized it for topiary; Renaissance gardens in Italy and France made it synonymous with elegance and structure. Today, over 90 species are recognized across temperate regions of Europe, Asia, Africa, and the Americas. It is, by any measure, a very successful shrub.

In the Mid-Atlantic United States, boxwood has been a garden staple for centuries, planted extensively throughout colonial-era estates and still thriving in historic landscapes across Virginia, Maryland, and D.C. The American Boxwood Society was founded in part to address threats from "boxwood decline" — a disease that devastated established plantings in Virginia during the mid-20th century. Today, boxwood blight remains an active concern, making the conservation work at Blandy and the National Arboretum more important than ever.

The collections featured on this site represent the gold standard in Buxus stewardship — places where history, horticulture, and science converge. One of them also has a Cornwallis memorial, which is not something most shrub collections can claim.
