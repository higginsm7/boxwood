About This Site

The genus Buxus has shaped formal gardens for over two thousand years. In the Mid-Atlantic, two collections stand out as premier destinations for enthusiasts and researchers alike — one a living reference library tucked into the Virginia countryside, the other a landmark of American botanical science in the heart of Washington, D.C.

Virginia

Boyce, Clarke County, VA

ABS Memorial Garden
at Blandy Experimental Farm

Founded in 1961 by a group of gardeners alarmed by the spread of boxwood decline throughout Virginia, the American Boxwood Society made its permanent home at Blandy's Orland E. White Arboretum. The ABS Memorial Garden — developed in 1974 as a tribute to Society founders Henry Hohman and Dr. John T. Baldwin — has grown into the largest reference collection of its kind, showcasing over 162 labeled cultivars across manicured beds on the arboretum's 172-acre grounds.

Washington, D.C.

Northeast Washington, D.C.

National Boxwood Collection
U.S. National Arboretum

Established by an act of Congress in 1927 and administered by the USDA's Agricultural Research Service, the U.S. National Arboretum's boxwood holdings are among the most comprehensive in the temperate world. The National Boxwood Collection comprises over 180 taxa — species, varieties, and cultivars — and serves as a critical resource for botanical research, plant breeding, and disease resistance studies. It also serves as a sister backup collection to the ABS Memorial Garden at Blandy.

Living Reference Libraries

Both gardens function as scientific reference collections — every plant labeled, documented, and available for study by researchers, breeders, and curious visitors alike.

Mutual Conservation

The Blandy and National Arboretum collections serve as mutual backup repositories, ensuring cultivar diversity is protected if disease threatens one site.

About Buxus

A Genus with
Deep Roots

Boxwood — genus Buxus — has been a cornerstone of formal garden design since antiquity. Ancient Romans prized it for topiary; Renaissance gardens in Italy and France made it synonymous with elegance and structure. Today, over 90 species are recognized across temperate regions of Europe, Asia, Africa, and the Americas.

In the Mid-Atlantic United States, boxwood has been a garden staple for centuries, planted extensively throughout colonial-era estates and still thriving in historic landscapes across Virginia, Maryland, and D.C. The American Boxwood Society was founded in part to address threats from "boxwood decline" — a disease that devastated established plantings in Virginia during the mid-20th century. Today, boxwood blight remains an active concern, making the conservation work at Blandy and the National Arboretum more important than ever.

The collections featured on this site represent the gold standard in Buxus stewardship — places where history, horticulture, and science converge.
